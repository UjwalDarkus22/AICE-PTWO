The Four Approaches

Where Does Behaviour Come From?

Each approach offers a different lens on human behaviour. Click any card to explore.

🧬
Biological Approach
Behaviour stems from the brain, hormones, and genetics.
Holzel et al. (2011) — Meditation & Grey Matter
Dement & Kleitman (1957) — REM Sleep & Dreaming
Hassett et al. (2008) — Monkey Toy Preferences
 💭
Cognitive Approach
Behaviour is shaped by how we think, perceive, and remember.
Andrade (2010) — Doodling & Memory
Baron-Cohen et al. (2001) — Theory of Mind
Pozzulo et al. — Eyewitness Identification
 📚
Learning Approach
Behaviour is acquired through conditioning and observation.

Saavedra & Silverman (2002) — Button Phobia
Fagen et al. — Elephant SPR Training
 👥
Social Approach
Behaviour is driven by other people and situational context.

Piliavin et al. (1969) — Subway Samaritan
Perry et al. — Oxytocin & Personal Space

Key Concepts

Essential Vocabulary

Master these terms — then test yourself with the Vocabulary Quiz!

🧠
Neuroplasticity
The brain's ability to physically change its structure in response to experience — even in adulthood.
😴
REM Sleep
Rapid Eye Movement sleep — the stage where vivid dreaming occurs. Brain activity resembles wakefulness.
🧬
Nature vs Nurture
The debate over whether behaviour is caused by biology (nature) or environment/learning (nurture).
⚡
EEG
Electroencephalograph — measures electrical brain activity. Used to identify sleep stages.
🤔
Theory of Mind
The ability to attribute independent mental states to others — understanding that people have different beliefs and feelings.
❌
False Positive
Incorrectly identifying an innocent person as a criminal in a line-up. Dangerous in criminal justice contexts.
🔄
Counterbalancing
Varying the order of conditions across participants to control for order effects like practice or fatigue.
🌍
Ecological Validity
The extent to which findings reflect real-world behaviour. Lab studies often have lower ecological validity.
🔗
Classical Conditioning
Learning by association — pairing a neutral stimulus with one that causes a natural response.
🎁
Operant Conditioning
Learning through consequences — reinforcement increases behaviour; punishment decreases it.
👀
Social Learning Theory
Bandura's theory that learning occurs by observing and imitating models — especially when they are reinforced.
🧩
Backward Chaining
Teaching the last step of a behaviour chain first, then progressively adding earlier steps.
🤖
Agentic State
Milgram's concept — viewing yourself as an agent of authority, surrendering personal moral responsibility.
🌊
Diffusion of Responsibility
In a group, each person feels less responsible for helping because they assume someone else will act.
💉
Oxytocin
The "love hormone" — influences bonding, trust, and social behaviour. Has differential effects based on empathy level.
🕵️
Double-Blind Design
Neither participant nor experimenter knows which condition is applied — controls both types of bias simultaneously.
